The Problem

Students usually face two major problems before choosing a major.

Some students don’t know which major to choose. Others choose a major but don’t fully understand what it will be like. In both cases, students often make decisions based on limited information and unrealistic expectations, which can lead to confusion once university begins.

Issue One

I don't know which major fits me.

Students may be interested in fields like engineering, computing, or science, but that doesn’t mean they know which major actually fits their interests, strengths, or way of learning.

  • They rely on prestige, salary expectations, or advice from others.
  • They don’t fully understand how majors differ from each other.
  • They need guidance, not just information .
Issue Two

I chose a major, but I don’t fully understand it yet.

Some students choose a major but enter university without knowing what the experience will actually be like, including the workload, difficulty, and type of work involved.

  • They underestimate how demanding the workload is
  • They don’t know what kind of work the major involves
  • They are not prepared for the academic difficulty

Why this is a real issue

Students often enter university with expectations that don’t match reality.

Students begin forming expectations about university before they actually experience it. Research by Crisp and colleagues shows that while students expect change, they often underestimate how different the workload and structure will feel.

A

Decisions are based on incomplete information

Research by Wiswall and Zafar shows that students often choose majors based on expected salary, job security, or social influence, rather than a clear understanding of what the major actually involves.

B

Expectations are often inaccurate

The same study by Wiswall and Zafar also found that students’ beliefs about majors are often incomplete or incorrect, which means many students begin with expectations that do not match reality.

Explore the research behind this
C

Mismatch creates real problems

Research by Oliver shows that when expectations do not match actual experience, people are more likely to feel dissatisfied. In the context of choosing a major, this means students can feel uncertain, frustrated, or disconnected when their expectations do not match reality.

Research-backed direction

The problem isn’t a lack of information. It’s a lack of clear guidance.

That’s why Major Lens focuses on helping students understand their options clearly before university begins. Instead of guessing, students can explore majors, understand what to expect, and get the right guidance early.
